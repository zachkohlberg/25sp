Description

This course serves as an introduction to the principles of design and development using object-oriented techniques such as inheritance, polymorphism and encapsulation. Students apply OO techniques to develop event-driven programs. Code craftsmanship is emphasized. Students also learn to apply and recognize design patterns for OO software and to use standard application development frameworks.

Objectives

SER120 and SER120L share objectives. Upon successful completion of both courses, students should be able to:

  • Analyze, design, and implement software applications using object-oriented (OO) techniques
    • Use UML class diagrams for designing OO Java applications
    • Use OO programming concepts such as encapsulation, inherence and polymorphism during design and development
    • Explain a subset of software design patterns and use these patterns during design and development
  • Design and develop applications that display and animate 2D shapes
  • Design and develop event-driven graphical Java applications
  • Use advanced Java APIs and collections

Labor-Based Grading

What is Labor-Based Grading?

Labor-based grading and contract grading are approaches to grading that try to emphasize the work you put into a class over the outcomes of that work. This grading system specifies what work you need to complete to earn a particular final grade, and individual assignments are not graded. You receive a final grade based on what work you complete rather than a weighted average of grades from assignments and exams.

There are plenty of articles online about labor-based grading if you want to read about it in more detail. Here's one that was easy to find and that I think explains the concept well: https://cep.barnard.edu/labor-based-grading. This article also links to an example grade contract. My implementation will differ, but my goals are largely the same.

Why Labor-Based Grading?

The author of the example grade contract shown in the Bernard College webpage gives an excellent explanation of why they prefer contract grading over traditional grades. I recommend reading that if you want to hear more about the motivation behind labor-based grading. I'll try to summarize why I dislike traditional grading systems and think this system addresses some (but not all) of the problems with grades.

  • Grades are not a good source of feedback. Grades provide little information on their own compared to comments and other qualitative feedback.
  • Grades punish you for mistakes. Whether intended or not, low grades act as a punishment. Mistakes are a necessary part of the learning process, and you should not be penalized for them. Giving lower grades to indicate mistakes or areas for improvement is counterproductive.
  • A labor-based grading system is more transparent than a weighted average. If you want a particular grade, then you know exactly which assignments you must complete for that grade.

FAQ

  • If I'm not graded on an assignment, does that mean I don't need to put any effort into it? I still review assignments and check that they were completed as directed. You should expect the projects to challenge you and require a significant amount of work to finish.
  • Why can't I get an A+? QU's grading system does not include an A+. The highest grade you can earn in any QU class is an A.
  • Why can't I get an A-, B+, B-, C+, or C-? I don't see a reason to include an option for every in-between grade. An A is the highest grade you can earn, a B is less demanding but still a good grade, and a C represents the minimum requirements to pass this class and meet the prerequisite for other classes.
  • Is there extra credit? No, there is not. Extra credit doesn't serve a purpose in this grading system.
  • Can I get partial credit on an assignment? There is no partial credit for late or incomplete assignments. You must complete the entire assignment, submit it on time, and submit it in the correct format to receive credit for it. If you're having trouble, then please ask me or a tutor for help.
  • Are there exams? Graded exams, quizzes, and similar assessments don't work with this grading system. There are no exams, not even a final exam. However, I may give ungraded quizzes as a way for you to check your understanding of topics we've covered.

If you have questions about the grading system that aren't answered here, then please don't hesitate to ask me about it! I want you to feel comfortable with the grading system and understand what's required to earn the grade you want.

General Advice

When in doubt, ask questions before you do something that may violate the AI policy!

Sharing Work

There are many legitimate reasons that you may want to publish or share your work on an assignment. To list a few examples:

  • Making code publicly available on a site like GitHub as part of a portfolio or to provided it as a learning resource for other programmers
  • Comparing your code with someone else's code to learn from their approach to solving the same problem
  • Asking for assistance or feedback while working on an assignment

I consider it important that you are allowed to share work for any legitimate reason, and this matters more to me than trying to punish or discourage sharing for illegitimate reasons. If you tell me that you shared work in good faith, then I'll take your word for it unless you give me a reason not to.

Academic Integrity Requirements

To comply with the AI policy when sharing work:

  • Be honest if asked who you shared your work with.
  • Do not share work with someone who has stated or implied that they plan to copy that work or otherwise use it to violate the AI policy.
  • When posting work online, do not post it in a manner that encourages others to copy it or otherwise violate the AI policy. For example, posting your projects and advertising them as the solutions to a class's programming assignments may imply that they were posted for other people to copy.

You have my permission to share any and all work you do in this class, and you will not be responsible for anyone else's misuse of that shared work as long as you follow the above rules.

Advice

  • Credit yourself as the author of any programs you write.
  • Don't share your work with someone unless you're confident that they don't plan on copying it. While I don't consider it your job to ensure other students don't cheat off you, I don't think it's hard to guess when someone just wants your work so they can copy it. You'll still be dragged into the AI case when they get caught, and it's not going to be a fun process for you.
  • If you tell me that you shared your work with another student, then you are not implicating the person who you shared it with. If you think they did violate the AI policy, then lying about your involvement is itself a violation and will not protect the other student from consequences. If you don't think they violated the AI policy, then there is no reason to deny having shared work with them.

Asking for Help

You are allowed to ask anyone for help with any assignment in this class unless that assignment's directions say otherwise. This means you can talk to me, other instructors, tutors, classmates, friends, relatives, etc. as long as they're willing to spend their time helping you. You can learn a lot by talking to other programmers, and I want to encourage you to ask for help as often as you need it.

Academic Integrity Requirements

To comply with the AI policy when receiving assistance:

  • Acknowledge anyone who provided significant assistance at the beginning of your program and note how they assisted. "Significant assistance" means anything that took more than a few minutes. If you're not sure whether someone's help qualified as significant, then play it safe and credit them.
  • When you need help with an assignment and are talking to someone more knowledgeable (such as a professor, tutor, or someone who already finished the assignment):
    • Do not copy or paraphrase the other person's code, and do not view their code while writing yours. If someone is helping you, then your job is to ask good questions and gain a better understanding of the problem you're trying to solve or the techniques required to solve it. Use that understanding to write your own code and solve the problem your way. There are numerous ways to write any program, even something as simple as Hello World.
    • Do not rely on the other person to tell you what to type or how to write your program. If they're explaining the solution and you're just copying down what they say, then you aren't writing your own code.
  • When you and another student are both working on the same assignment and assisting each other:
    • Make sure you are both writing your own separate programs. You should not be writing one program together, copying each other, or otherwise sharing code. Each person's program must be entirely their own work. Being allowed to ask other people for help does not mean everything is a group project.
  • When asking for feedback or assistance with debugging:
    • The other person should not be fixing your code for you. You can ask them to help you find the problem, explain why something isn't working, or explain how to fix your program, but you should be the one fixing it. You need to understand the problem so that you can more easily recognize and fix it on your own in the future.

To summarize: whether or not someone else is helping you, you always need to complete your own work. Allowing someone to do your work for you or using their work in your assignment is a violation of the AI policy.

Advice

  • Don't ask someone for their code when you're struggling to write yours. Seeing a solution will just bias you towards imitating what they did, which will qualify as copying, cheating, and/or plagiarism. Possessing another person's work in this class does not violate the AI policy on its own, but it's unlikely to help you do anything other than cheat.
  • If you aren't sure whether you relied too much on the other person's assistance when coding, then try rewriting that part of the program from scratch (and don't just try to recreate the previous solution from memory). If you understand how to solve the problem and apply the relevant programming concepts, then this should be easy. When writing a program, most of the work is understanding a problem and designing a solution, not writing it down.
  • It's not the other person's responsibility to avoid providing too much assistance; it's your responsibility to do your own work. You need to convey to them what you're having trouble with and how you want them to help.

Group Assignments

Some assignments may allow for group work on all or part of the assignment. This is not the default, and any assignment that allows group work will state that group work is allowed and on which parts you may work as a group. If an assignment doesn't mention group work, then it's not group work.

Academic Integrity Requirements

To comply with the AI policy when working on group assignments:

  • All group members must be credited on the assignment. It must be clear that they are members of the group and co-authors of the work being submitted, as opposed to someone who assisted (see Asking for Help) or a source whose work was quoted or used in the assignment (see Using Other Sources). Failing to credit a group member violates the university's AI policy.
  • All group members must make significant contributions to a group assignment, and work must be divided reasonably evenly among all group members. If someone does significantly less work than the rest of the group, then they cannot be counted as part of the group. Crediting someone for work they did not do violates the university's AI policy.
  • Keep track of everyone's contributions. If a group needs to remove a member, then that member's work can no longer be used in the project. If you leave a group or the group dissolves entirely, then you are no longer allowed to use any work contributed by the other members.
  • All group members are responsible for communicating with one another and for all work submitted by the group. You should be familiar with the entire project and comfortable having your name attached to it. If this group work violates the AI policy (for example, if part of it is plagiarized), then every group member may bear some responsibility depending on the circumstances.

Advice

  • If you choose to work with someone else on a project, then you are choosing to be partially responsible for that person. Don't take this lightly, and don't continue working with someone if they violate your trust or if you think their behavior will cause problems for you.
  • Pay attention to which parts of a project are group work and which are not. A project may have some group components and some individual components.

Using Other Sources

By default, all work submitted in this class must be entirely your own work. This section covers the requirements for using work that is not yours in an assignment.

Academic Integrity Requirements

First, the following are always acceptable:

  • Code or other material intentionally provided with an assignment can be used freely, and you don't need to include any credit for this material.
    • If material was obviously included by accident (for example, an entire solution was posted with the assignment), then submitting the material as though it was your own work may be treated as cheating or plagiarism.
    • If material was included by accident (obvious or not), then I will remove the material as soon as I am aware of it and notify the class. If you used the material assuming it was posted intentionally, then you will be allowed to update and resubmit the assignment without that material.
  • If I provide material outside of an assignment, such as my notes or an in-class example, then you are free to use it in any assignment for this class. The material should still be credited as mentioned below.

Otherwise, to comply with the AI policy when using other sources:

  • If copying, paraphrasing, or basing your work on material from another source:
    • You must have my written permission (an email is sufficient) to use that material in your assignment. I may grant permission in the assignment directions, but if not then you'll need to ask me ahead of time.
    • All work that is not your own must be clearly credited. You must specify which part(s) of the assignment are from another source, which source it was from, and link to or otherwise provide a means to check the original source.
  • If you are using another source for help without copying, paraphrasing, or basing your work on the material in that source:
    • You must credit the source as you would credit someone who provided assistance, as noted in the Asking for Help section.

Last, a note about "double-dipping" and self-plagiarism:

To quote the university's AI policy, double-dipping refers to "multiple uses of the same work" or "presenting the same or substantially the same written work (or portion thereof) as part of the course requirement for more than one project or course, without the express prior written permission of the instructor(s) involved." I'm usually okay with you reusing work in other classes or using work from another class in my class, but you still need written permission from both me and the other instructor.

If you are retaking this class, then I expect you to complete every assignment from scratch without reusing any of your old work. I consider reusing work from the last time you took this class to be another form of double-dipping.

Artificial Intelligence

While I don't advise using services marketed as "artificial intelligence" to assist with work for classes, especially introductory classes, I'll save that for the advice section. You're free to use these services as long as you follow the rules given below.

To avoid confusion, I'll refer to any artificial intelligence service as an LLM (large language model, which describes ChatGPT and similar services currently available) and continue using AI to abbreviate "academic integrity."

Academic Integrity Requirements

To comply with the AI policy when using an LLM:

  • You must use an interface that keeps a record of your conversation and allows you to share a link to the entire conversation. For example, https://chatgpt.com (as of 2025/01/08) includes a "Share" button near the top-right of the conversation panel. Ask the LLM to state the current date at the beginning of this conversation to verify when the interaction took place. You may be asked to share the conversation again if this use of the LLM is suspected to violate the academic integrity policy, so don't use it to discuss anything that you're not comfortable sharing later. If you cannot provide a link to the conversation record, then you are not permitted to use it to assist with any assignments in this class.
  • You must state at the beginning of your program that you used an LLM to assist you when working on the assignment, summarize how it assisted you, and include a link to the entire conversation(s) in which this assistance took place (see above).
  • You may not use any material (code, text, images, etc.) generated by the LLM in your work. Any use of the LLM must follow the guidelines given in other sections regarding Asking for Help and Using Other Sources.

Advice

I recommend against any use of LLMs in this class for the following reasons:

  • An LLM is not intelligent and does not understand the conversation you are having with it. It is prone to hallucinating (making up false information), and it does not know or care when it is hallucinating. As a tool, it is most useful when you have the expertise to verify factual claims and code generated by the LLM. Don't rely on an LLM to explain something to you if you don't understand the topic well enough to critically evaluate its explanation.
  • When learning to program or learning new programming concepts, you have to start practicing with relatively small and simple problems. An LLM has seen hundreds of thousands of examples like these and can easily give you a working solution. If you could learn a new skill by just viewing the solutions to problems, then we would give you the solutions instead of asking you to create them yourself. You will not learn unless you practice solving problems, and you will not be able to attempt the more complex problems if you rely on an LLM to solve the simple problems for you.
  • The point of this class is to teach you about computer science and software engineering topics. This is not a course on "prompt engineering". If you believe that an LLM will be crucial to your future career as a programmer, then becoming an expert in programming will put you in the best position to utilize an LLM in that career.

Academic Integrity Violations

This section covers how I typically handle AI violations. The full procedure is covered in the university's AI policy, so this will just cover details relevant to our class and my own habits.

Investigation

If I believe that you have committed an AI violation or that your work was used in an AI violation, then I will contact you to ask for more information after I've gathered the necessary evidence. I don't like to pursue suspected violations unless I see conclusive evidence that a violation took place, so you are not likely to convince me of anything by lying at this point.

If you did not violate the AI policy, then this is your opportunity to clear any suspicion by providing additional evidence and letting me know about any work you have shared with other students. Knowing the original author of plagiarized work allows me to argue that the original author does not bear responsibility for the violation due to the Sharing Work section of this syllabus. If you decide to lie about sharing work and later claim to have shared that work in good faith, then I will not believe you.

If you have violated the AI policy, then it's probably too late to avoid getting in trouble once I contact you about the violation. You can cooperate, in which case we can discuss the AI process and attempt a joint resolution (if allowed) after I file the report. If you appear to be lying or decide not to cooperate, then I'm not going to continue talking to you about the violation and will not likely consider a joint resolution, even if later decide to accept responsibility for the violation.

Consequences for AI Violations

This section covers what I believe are appropriate consequences on a first offense for most AI violations. The university's AI policy explains the procedure for determining consequences.

  • If some or all of an assignment you submitted wasn't your own work, then you should not receive any credit for the assignment or be allowed to resubmit it. You would not normally receive credit for completing only part of the assignment without cheating, so to give any credit would be a reward for the AI violation. This is to remain consistent with my grading policy, and I do not view it as the primary sanction.
  • Your final grade should be further reduced by one letter per violation of the AI policy. For this purpose, I would treat cheating on two assignments or helping someone cheat on two assignments to be two separate violations, and as a result this would lower your final grade by two letters.
  • If the above sanction would lower your grade below an F, then you should instead receive a final grade of FAI.
